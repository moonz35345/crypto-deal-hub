What Is Crypto Staking?

Staking is the process of locking up your cryptocurrency to help secure a blockchain network. In return, you earn rewards — typically paid in the same coin you staked. Think of it like earning interest on a savings account, but for crypto.

Unlike Bitcoin, which uses energy-intensive mining to validate transactions, proof-of-stake (PoS) networks like Ethereum, Solana, and Cardano rely on users locking up tokens as collateral. The more tokens staked, the more secure the network becomes.

💡 Key Insight

You don't need to be a technical expert to staking. Most major exchanges now offer one-click staking, and DeFi protocols make it accessible with just a wallet and a few clicks. Average APYs range from 3% to 15% depending on the asset and platform.

How Staking Works

When you stake crypto, you're essentially delegating your tokens to a validator — a computer that processes transactions and creates new blocks on the blockchain. Validators are chosen based on how many tokens they have staked. If they act honestly, they earn rewards. If they misbehave, a portion of their stake gets "slashed" (destroyed).

As a delegator, you share in the validator's rewards minus a commission fee (typically 5-15%). You never give up custody of your tokens with non-custodial staking — you're just delegating your voting power.

Types of Staking

  • Exchange staking (CeFi) — You stake directly through an exchange like Coinbase or Kraken. Easiest option, but the exchange controls your tokens.
  • DeFi staking (non-custodial) — You stake directly on the blockchain through a protocol like Lido or Rocket Pool. You keep custody of your tokens, but need your own wallet.
  • Liquid staking — You receive a "receipt token" (e.g., stETH for ETH) that represents your staked position. This receipt token can be used in DeFi while your original tokens earn rewards.
  • Institutional staking — Platforms like Figment or Cobo serve large staking operations with enterprise-grade infrastructure.

⚠️ Important

Staked tokens are typically locked during an "unbonding period" — ranging from instant (exchange staking) to 21 days (native Ethereum staking). During this time, your tokens can't be sold or transferred. Liquid staking solves this by giving you a tradeable receipt token.

Step-by-Step: How to Stake Crypto

  1. Choose a staking method
    For beginners, exchange staking is the easiest. Create an account on a platform like Kraken or Coinbase, and look for the "Staking" or "Earn" section. For more control, use a non-custodial option like Lido.
  2. Select the asset you want to stake
    Not all cryptocurrencies can be staked. Proof-of-work coins like Bitcoin can't be staked (you'd need to mine instead). The most popular staking assets are Ethereum (ETH), Solana (SOL), Cardano (ADA), Polkadot (DOT), and Cosmos (ATOM).
  3. Compare APY rates
    Rewards vary by platform and asset. Exchange staking typically offers 3-8% APY, while DeFi liquid staking can range from 3-5% for ETH but much higher for smaller assets. Always compare rates across platforms — even a 1% difference compounds significantly over time.
  4. Start staking
    On an exchange, this is usually as simple as clicking "Stake" next to the asset in your wallet and confirming the amount. On DeFi protocols, you'll connect your wallet (like MetaMask), approve the transaction, and confirm the stake. Gas fees apply on Ethereum.
  5. Track your rewards
    Most platforms display your accumulated staking rewards in real-time. Rewards are typically distributed daily or weekly. You can often restake your rewards automatically to compound your returns — this is the most powerful way to grow your holdings over time.

Exchange Staking vs. DeFi Staking: Which Is Better?

Exchange staking is the simplest approach. You click a button and start earning. The downside is that the exchange controls your tokens — if the exchange gets hacked or freezes withdrawals, your staked assets are at risk. This is "Not your keys, not your coins."

DeFi staking gives you full control. Your tokens stay in your own wallet, and you interact directly with smart contracts. The trade-off is more complexity — you need a non-custodial wallet, you'll pay gas fees, and you're responsible for your own security.

Our recommendation: For amounts under $5,000, exchange staking on Kraken is perfectly fine. For larger holdings, consider non-custodial options like Lido or Rocket Pool for better security.

Which Coins Can You Stake?

Only proof-of-stake (PoS) cryptocurrencies can be staked. Here are the most popular options:

  • Ethereum (ETH) — The largest PoS network. Requires 32 ETH to run your own validator, but you can stake any amount through exchanges or liquid staking protocols. APY: 3-4.5%.
  • Solana (SOL) — Fast-growing network with higher rewards. APY: 5-7%. Easy to stake through exchanges or Phantom wallet's built-in staking.
  • Cardano (ADA) — One of the original PoS coins. No lock-up period — you keep full liquidity while staking. APY: 3-5%.
  • Polkadot (DOT) — Nominated proof-of-stake system. Requires 350+ DOT to stake natively, but exchanges allow any amount. APY: 8-14%.
  • Cosmos (ATOM) — The "internet of blockchains." APY: 12-18%, one of the highest among major PoS coins.
  • Polygon (MATIC/POL) — Ethereum scaling solution. APY: 4-6%.

Risks of Staking

Staking is not risk-free. Before you stake, understand these potential downsides:

  • Slashing — If the validator you delegate to misbehaves, a portion of your staked tokens can be destroyed. This risk is minimized by choosing reputable validators or exchanges.
  • Lock-up periods — Your tokens may be locked for days or weeks. During this time, you can't sell even if the price crashes.
  • Market risk — If the crypto price drops 30% while you're earning 5% APY, you're still down 25%. Staking rewards don't protect against price declines.
  • Smart contract risk — DeFi staking relies on code that could contain bugs or be exploited. Stick to well-audited protocols.
  • Platform risk — If the exchange or protocol gets hacked, your staked assets could be lost. Diversify across platforms.

⚠️ What About the Aave rsETH Incident?

In April 2026, the Kelp DAO rsETH exploit highlighted how quickly DeFi risks can move between protocols. An attacker created 116,500 unbacked rsETH (~$292M) using a LayerZero bridge exploit, then used the unbacked tokens as collateral to borrow real assets from Aave. While Aave itself wasn't hacked, it illustrates why protocol audits and diversification matter. Stick to battle-tested platforms and never stake more than you can afford to lose.

Tax Implications

Staking rewards are generally treated as taxable income in most jurisdictions. Here's a brief overview:

  • United States: Staking rewards are taxed as ordinary income at fair market value when you receive them. The cost basis for future sales is the value at the time of receipt.
  • United Kingdom: Staking income is subject to Income Tax. If you later sell the rewarded tokens, Capital Gains Tax applies on the profit.
  • European Union: Treatment varies by country. In most EU nations, staking rewards are taxed as income at receipt, with potential capital gains on disposal.

Always consult a tax professional for advice specific to your situation. This is not tax advice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I lose money when staking crypto?▼
Yes, through two mechanisms: (1) Slashing — if your chosen validator behaves maliciously, part of your stake can be destroyed. (2) Market risk — if the token price drops significantly, your staking rewards may not offset the loss. However, staking rewards do provide a partial buffer against price declines.
What's the difference between staking and lending?▼
Staking involves locking assets to secure a blockchain network and earn protocol rewards. Lending involves providing assets to borrowers through DeFi protocols like Aave. Both generate yield, but have different risk profiles. Staking risk is primarily slashing and lock-up periods, while lending risk is smart contract bugs and borrower defaults.
How much can I earn from staking?▼
It depends on the asset and platform. As of June 2026: ETH staking earns 3-4.5%, SOL earns 5-7%, ADA earns 3-5%, and ATOM earns 12-18%. These are variable rates that change based on network participation. Over a year, a $10,000 stake at 5% APY would earn approximately $500 in rewards.
Do I need a hardware wallet for staking?▼
Not required, but recommended for large holdings. Exchange staking doesn't need any special hardware — you just use the exchange's app. For DeFi staking, any non-custodial wallet works (MetaMask, Phantom, Ledger). If you're staking significant amounts (over $10,000), a hardware wallet like Ledger or Trezor significantly improves security.
Is staking safe on exchanges?▼
Major exchanges like Kraken and Coinbase have strong security and are generally safe for staking. The risk is custodial — the exchange controls your tokens. For maximum security, use non-custodial staking with a hardware wallet. For most beginners, the convenience of exchange staking outweighs the custodial risk.
Can I unstake anytime?▼
It depends on the platform and asset. Exchange staking (like Kraken) often allows instant or near-instant unstaking for ETH. Native Ethereum staking requires going through an unbonding queue (currently 1-5 days). Solana unbonding takes ~2 epochs (roughly 2-4 days). Cardano has no unbonding period — you can unstake instantly.
