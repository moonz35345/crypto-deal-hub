Crypto Tax Guide 2026: How Cryptocurrency Is Taxed (US, UK, EU)

By the CryptoDealHub TeamUpdated: June 2026📝 Recently Updated11 min read

Tax rules for cryptocurrency vary by country but the core principles are universal. This guide covers what's taxable, how to calculate gains, and how to report crypto taxes in the US, UK, and EU.

What Crypto Events Are Taxable?

Not every crypto transaction triggers a tax event. Here's a clear breakdown:

  • Taxable: Selling crypto for fiat (USD, GBP, EUR)
  • Taxable: Trading one crypto for another (e.g., ETH → SOL)
  • Taxable: Using crypto to buy goods or services
  • Taxable: Receiving staking rewards, airdrops, or mining income
  • Taxable: Receiving crypto as payment for work
  • Not taxable: Buying crypto with fiat and holding it
  • Not taxable: Transferring crypto between your own wallets
  • Not taxable: Gifting crypto (under annual gift limits)
  • Not taxable: Donating crypto to qualified charities (may be deductible)

Capital Gains Tax Explained

When you sell or trade crypto for a profit, that profit is a capital gain. If you sell for less than you paid, it's a capital loss — which can offset your gains and reduce your tax bill.

How to calculate your gain:

  1. Determine your cost basis (what you paid for the crypto, including fees)
  2. Determine your sale price (what you received, minus fees)
  3. Subtract cost basis from sale price = your capital gain or loss

Example: You buy 1 ETH for $2,000 and sell it for $3,500. Your capital gain is $1,500. Depending on your jurisdiction and how long you held it, this gain is taxed at either short-term or long-term rates.

Short-Term vs. Long-Term

Most jurisdictions differentiate between assets held for less than a year (short-term, taxed at higher income rates) and those held for more than a year (long-term, taxed at lower capital gains rates). In the US, short-term gains are taxed at your ordinary income rate (up to 37%), while long-term gains are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your total income.

Income Tax on Crypto Earnings

Crypto received as income — whether from staking, airdrops, mining, or employment — is taxed as ordinary income at its fair market value on the day you receive it. This means:

  • Staking rewards: Taxed as income when credited to your account. The value at receipt becomes your cost basis for future sales.
  • Airdrops: Taxed as income when you gain control over the tokens (usually when they appear in your wallet).
  • Mining: Taxed as income at fair market value when mined. If mining is a business, self-employment tax may also apply.
  • DeFi yield/interest: Taxed as income when received.

Staking & Airdrop Taxation

Staking rewards are one of the most common tax events for crypto holders. Here's how they work:

  • You stake 1 ETH and receive 0.04 ETH in rewards over a year
  • When each reward is credited, you owe income tax on its USD value at that moment
  • If the reward was worth $80 when received, you report $80 as income
  • When you later sell that 0.04 ETH, you pay capital gains tax on any appreciation from the $80 cost basis

Airdrops follow the same principle — the fair market value at receipt is taxable income. If you receive an airdrop worth $200, you report $200 as income regardless of whether you've sold it yet.

United States

The IRS treats cryptocurrency as property. Every sale, trade, or spend is a taxable event. Key points for US taxpayers:

  • Reporting: Use Form 8949 to report each transaction, then transfer totals to Schedule D
  • Question on Form 1040: You must answer the crypto question on your tax return — even if you didn't trade
  • Short-term gains: Taxed at ordinary income rates (10%-37%)
  • Long-term gains: Taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% based on income
  • Net Investment Income Tax: Additional 3.8% on investment income above $200K (single) / $250K (married)
  • Loss harvesting: You can deduct up to $3,000 in net capital losses per year against ordinary income, carrying forward excess losses

United Kingdom

HMRC treats crypto as a capital asset for individuals. Key points for UK taxpayers:

  • Capital Gains Tax: Applies to profits above the annual exempt amount (£3,000 for 2025/26)
  • Tax rates: 10% (basic rate taxpayers) or 20% (higher rate taxpayers) on crypto gains
  • Income Tax: Staking, mining, and airdrop rewards are taxed as income at your marginal rate (20%-45%)
  • Bed and breakfasting rule: You can't sell crypto and immediately rebuy the same asset to realize a loss — you must wait 30 days
  • Reporting: Report through Self Assessment tax return

Record Keeping & Tools

Good record keeping is essential for accurate tax reporting. Track every transaction including:

  • Date and time of each transaction
  • Amount of crypto bought/sold/traded
  • Price in your local currency at the time of transaction
  • Fees paid
  • Wallet addresses and exchange records

Recommended tools: CoinTracker, Koinly, TokenTax, and CoinLedger can automatically sync with exchanges and wallets to generate tax reports. These tools connect to your exchange via API and categorize transactions automatically, saving hours of manual work.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I owe taxes if I haven't sold my crypto?▼
No. Simply buying and holding cryptocurrency is not a taxable event. You only owe taxes when you sell, trade, spend, or earn crypto. However, staking rewards and airdrops received while holding are taxable as income even if you haven't sold anything.
How are crypto-to-crypto trades taxed?▼
In most jurisdictions, trading one crypto for another (e.g., ETH → SOL) is a taxable event. You're deemed to have "sold" the ETH at its fair market value and "bought" the SOL at that same value. If the ETH had appreciated since you bought it, you owe capital gains tax on the profit.
Can I deduct crypto losses?▼
Yes, in most jurisdictions. Capital losses can offset capital gains. In the US, you can deduct up to $3,000 in net losses against ordinary income per year, carrying forward excess losses to future years. The UK allows losses to offset gains but requires them to be reported within 4 years.
Are NFTs taxed differently?▼
NFTs are generally treated the same as other crypto assets for tax purposes. Buying an NFT with crypto is a taxable disposal of that crypto. Selling an NFT for a profit triggers capital gains tax. Creating and selling NFTs may be treated as business income. The specific treatment varies by jurisdiction.
What if I forgot to report crypto taxes from previous years?▼
File amended returns as soon as possible. In the US, use Form 1040-X. The IRS has generally been more lenient with voluntary corrections than with discovered non-compliance. Many tax professionals recommend proactive disclosure to minimize penalties. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.
